    • BACKGROUND

    Over the past two years, I have been exploring using distances between exoplanets as pitch material for new tuning systems, inspired by the ancient concept of musica universalis or “music of the spheres” interpreting the movement of celestial bodies (traditionally those within our own solar system) as music. The intention is to represent and evoke through music, these interesting newly-discovered planetary systems.


    METHOD

    I derived my method from Hasler and Godwin’s categorisations and descriptions of attempts throughout history to map pitches to planets. My approach deviates from the traditional “geocentric” method which uses Earth as the center, instead placing the host star at the center (heliocentric) and then using frequencies scaled from the distance between planets (Type A) or the time it takes them to orbit the star (Type B).

    This provides two unique sets of notes to explore for each star system. The character of these sets notes varies widely with some happening to be close to traditional western notes and scales, some being far from it and many in-between. Tuning files can be created using these notes, to be used with electronic instruments to automatically map keyboards and sequencers to new tunings. You can download and try out some of the tunings that I've created here: Tunings

    Further reading on Musica Universalis:

    Godwin, J. (1995) Harmonies of heaven and earth: Mysticism in music from antiquity to the avant-garde. Rochester, VT: Inner Traditions International.

    Hasler, J. (2011) Towards Hermeticist Grammars of Music: A Proposal for Systems of Composition Based on the Principles of the Hermetic Tradition, with Musical Demonstrations. University of Newcastle upon Tyne.

    MUSIC OF THE 55 CANCRI BINARY STAR SYSTEM

    This composition, for cello and modular synthesizer, is based on 55 Cancri- a star system 41 light years away, within the constellation of Cancer. The star system features five planets- Galileo, Brahe, Lipperhey, Janssen and Harriot with varying properties such as Janssen’s oceans of lava and Harriot- a gas giant with potentially habitable moons.

    View Score

    Part 1 features frequencies derived from the distances between the planets, so the lowest note representing the lava planet Janssen which is closest to the system's two stars. Part 2 is an interpretation of a starscape. Part 3 features frequencies derived from the orbital speed of the planets, so here the lowest note represents Lipperhey- the planet furthest from the system's two stars.


    This piece was performed live at the Church of St Thomas the Martyr, Bristol 27th July 2025 and features cello by Liz Muir from Dead Space Chamber Music.
